How Bloop Maker works

Bloop Maker writes original video game music by following music-theory rules, not by running a trained AI model. Here is exactly what happens, in plain English, every time you press Generate.

Most "music generators" today are AI models trained on huge piles of existing songs, predicting what note comes next. Bloop Maker is different. It is a procedural music generator: it builds a song from deterministic rules about how music works, the same way a game builds a dungeon from rules about rooms and corridors. No neural network, no training data, no internet. The result is music that's genuinely yours, with no copyright or "is this AI?" cloud over it.

Here's the whole pipeline, step by step. You don't need to know any of this to use the app, but if you're curious, here it is.

✦ ✦ ✦

1. It starts with a seed

Every song begins with a single number called a seed, which makes the whole song deterministic: the same seed and settings always produce the exact same song, note for note. That's why you can copy a seed, send it to a friend, and they hear precisely what you heard.

2. It picks a key, a scale, and chords

Bloop Maker chooses a key and a scale (major, minor, dorian, mixolydian, lydian, harmonic minor, pentatonic, or whole-tone, each with its own mood). Then it builds a chord progression from tables of progressions known to sound good in that scale, alternating between two of them and varying the harmonic rhythm so it never feels stuck.

3. It writes a motif, the hook

A motif is the short melodic idea you'd hum after one listen. Bloop Maker generates one, then scores it for quality (good contour, no awkward leaps) and retries if it's weak. That quality-gate is a big reason the output sounds intentional rather than random.

3.5. It commits to a signature rhythm

A real song has a consistent rhythmic identity, like a short-short-long pattern. Bloop Maker locks in one signature rhythm and reuses it, so even as the notes change, the feel stays recognizable.

4. It remembers and repeats phrases

This is the secret ingredient. A melody that never repeats feels lost. Bloop Maker caches the first four-bar phrase and replays it in later sections, transposed to the current chord, with small variations each time. This "phrase memory" is what makes a generated song actually catchy.

5. It plans a song structure

Bloop Maker lays out intro, build, verse, chorus, break, and outro and treats each differently: the chorus opens up and gets louder, the intro and outro pull back, the bass shifts from a held note to a walking line. There's a planned high point and a drum fill before each new section, so the song feels like it's going somewhere.

6. It fills in every instrument, bar by bar

With the chords, motif, rhythm, and structure set, Bloop Maker writes the notes for each channel (lead, harmony, bass, drums, arpeggio, pad), applying music-theory rules so the parts fit together:

  • Chord-tone snapping: important notes on strong beats are pulled onto notes that belong to the current chord, so nothing clashes.
  • Leap recovery: after a big jump in pitch, the melody steps back the other way, the way singers naturally do.
  • Ornaments and suspensions: little grace notes and "lean-and-resolve" moments add expression.
  • Call and response: when the lead pauses, the harmony answers in the gap, like two voices talking.
  • Countermelody: in the chorus a second line moves opposite to the lead, making it feel full.

7. It polishes the whole thing

Finally, a set of finishing passes run over the entire song:

  • Chromatic approach notes, tiny "blue notes" that slide into a chord tone for flavor.
  • Modal interchange, an occasional "borrowed" chord that adds emotional color without leaving the key.
  • Drum fills right before section changes, so transitions feel intentional.
  • A dynamics curve across the whole song so it swells and breathes instead of staying flat.

What the rules are based on

Bloop Maker is a rule-based generator, not an AI model. Its rules are built from music theory from free and publicly available academic and educational sources, never from training on a library of songs. The rules are grounded in two things:

  • Music theory, the same principles taught in any classroom: which chords lead into others, how melodies rise and fall, and how a phrase asks a question and answers it. Music theory principles
  • The conventions of game music: the patterns the genre is known for, like melodies that mostly step to a nearby note, short repeating hooks, and tight, punchy rhythms, turned into weighted choices.

Because it's rule-based and deterministic rather than trained on existing songs, the music is genuinely original. It isn't remixing anyone's recordings, so there's no copyright-claim risk and nothing for a platform to flag as AI content.

Why rules instead of AI?

Three reasons. Ownership: the music comes from code, not a model trained on other people's songs, so it's yours to use commercially. Control: rules are steerable, push a song bright, dark, cozy, punchy, cinematic, or lo-fi with one button, and the same seed always gives the same result. Independence: instant generation, no server, no subscription, no internet, it all runs on your own computer.

What kinds of songs can it make?

A wide range of moods: calm ambient, town and exploration themes, fast battle music, plus jazzy, funky, silly, and weird flavors, across nine scales and six steering modes. It's built for chiptune and video game music, but the same engine makes menu themes, boss fights, cutscene music, and cozy shop loops. And since it exports standard MIDI, you can take any idea into your own software and turn it into any style.

Bloop Maker vs AI music generators

AI generators are trained on huge libraries of songs and predict the next note, so the output can echo what it was trained on, usually needs an account or subscription, and is hard to control. Bloop Maker is the opposite: rule-based and deterministic, so the music is original and yours; fully offline as a one-time purchase; and steerable and editable instead of endless prompt based rerolls. For creators who need music that's safe to ship, that's the whole point.

What you do with the result

Edit it note by note in the piano roll, reshape parts with one-click tools (simplify, swing, harmonize, reverse, and more), regenerate any single channel, then export as a standard MIDI file for any DAW or render to WAV with your own soundfonts.

Hearing your songs: using a SoundFont

Bloop Maker makes the music; a SoundFont (.sf2) provides the instrument sounds. To hear audio, drag a .sf2 file into the Soundfont box (or click to browse) and press play. Swap soundfonts anytime to change the vibe, from 8-bit bleeps to a full orchestra, and in the full version each channel can have its own and you can use multiple soundfonts.

Plenty of high-quality SoundFonts are free. Good ones to start with:

  • Arachno SoundFont (General MIDI, Roland GS)
  • GeneralUser GS (General MIDI, Roland GS)
  • Roland SC-55 (classic game-console sound)

You can also browse many more on Musical Artifacts and Soundfonts 4U.

One important note: the music you generate is yours, but each SoundFont is made by someone else and carries its own license. Exporting MIDI is always fine, but if you render audio (WAV) for a commercial project, check the license of the soundfont you used first. Full details on soundfont licensing here.
