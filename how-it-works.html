How Bloop Maker works

Bloop Maker writes original video game music by following music-theory rules, not by running a trained AI model. Here is exactly what happens, in plain English, every time you press Generate.

Most "music generators" you see today are AI models: they're trained on huge piles of existing songs and try to predict what note comes next. Bloop Maker works in a completely different way. It is a procedural music generator, it builds a song from a set of deterministic rules about how music is put together, the same way a video game procedurally builds a dungeon from rules about rooms and corridors. There is no neural network, no training data, and no internet involved. The result is music that is genuinely yours, with no copyright or "is this AI?" cloud hanging over it.

Below is the whole pipeline, step by step. None of this requires you to understand music theory to use the app, but if you're curious what's happening under the hood, here it is.

1. It starts with a seed

Every song begins with a single number called a seed. The seed feeds a pseudo-random number generator, which means the whole song is deterministic: the same seed and the same settings will always produce the exact same song, note for note. That's why you can copy a seed, send it to a friend, and they hear precisely what you heard. It also means nothing is ever truly "random chaos", it's reproducible, controllable randomness.

2. It picks a key, a scale, and chords

Next, Bloop Maker chooses a musical key (the home note) and a scale, the family of notes the song is allowed to use, like major (bright and happy), minor (darker and sadder), dorian, mixolydian, lydian, harmonic minor, pentatonic, or whole-tone. Each scale has its own emotional flavor.

From there it builds a chord progression, the sequence of chords the song moves through, by pulling from tables of progressions that are known to sound good in that scale. To keep things from getting repetitive, it actually prepares two progressions and alternates between them across the song. It also varies the harmonic rhythm: sometimes a chord holds for two bars to feel calm, sometimes chords change every bar to feel busy.

3. It writes a motif, the hook

A motif is a short, memorable melodic idea, the few notes you'd hum after hearing a song once. Bloop Maker generates a small motif of a handful of notes, then scores it for quality using rules about good melodic shape (does it have a nice contour? does it avoid awkward leaps?). If the motif scores poorly, it throws it away and tries again. This quality-gate is a big part of why the output sounds intentional rather than random.

3.5. It commits to a signature rhythm

One thing that separates a real song from random notes is a consistent rhythmic identity, think of the short-short-long pattern in many famous themes. Bloop Maker locks in one signature rhythm at the start and reuses it throughout the melody. So even as the notes change, the feel stays recognizable.

4. It remembers and repeats phrases

This is the secret ingredient. A melody that wanders forever without repeating doesn't feel like a song, it feels lost. Bloop Maker caches the first four-bar phrase it writes and then replays it in later matching sections, transposed to fit whatever chord is playing. Each repeat gets small, deliberate variations, a slightly different ending, a layer of harmony, a shift up an octave, so it stays interesting without losing the hook. This "phrase memory" is what makes a generated song actually catchy.

5. It plans a song structure

Real songs have an arc. Bloop Maker lays out sections, intro, build, verse, chorus, break, and outro, and treats each one differently. The chorus opens up into a higher pitch range and gets louder; the intro and outro pull back; the bass switches from a calm held note in the intro to a walking, moving line in the chorus. There's a planned high point (a "money note") and a transition fill on the drums right before a new section kicks in. All of this gives the song a sense of going somewhere.

6. It fills in every instrument, bar by bar

With the chords, motif, rhythm, and structure decided, Bloop Maker writes the actual notes for each of its channels, lead melody, harmony, bass, drums, arpeggio, and pad, one bar at a time. Along the way it applies a stack of music-theory rules so the parts sound good together:

  • Chord-tone snapping: important notes on strong beats are pulled onto notes that belong to the current chord, so nothing clashes.
  • Leap recovery: after a big jump in pitch, the melody steps back the other way, the way singers naturally do.
  • Ornaments and suspensions: little grace notes and "lean-and-resolve" moments add expression.
  • Call and response: when the lead pauses, the harmony answers in the gap, like two voices talking.
  • Countermelody: in the chorus a second line moves opposite to the lead, making it feel full.

7. It polishes the whole thing

Finally, a set of finishing passes run over the entire song:

  • Chromatic approach notes, tiny "blue notes" that slide into a chord tone for flavor.
  • Modal interchange, an occasional "borrowed" chord that adds emotional color without leaving the key.
  • Drum fills right before section changes, so transitions feel intentional.
  • A dynamics curve across the whole song so it swells and breathes instead of staying flat.

What the rules are based on

Bloop Maker is a rule-based generator, not an AI model. It was never trained on a library of songs, and there is no machine-learning model running when you press Generate. It follows a set of explicit, deterministic music-theory rules to build each track. The same input always produces the same output, and every rule is a concrete piece of logic, not a hidden weight buried inside a black box.

Those rules are grounded in two things.

First, music theory. Centuries of well-understood principles about how music works: which chords lead naturally into other chords, how melodies rise and fall, which notes resolve tension, and how a phrase can ask a musical question and then answer it. This is the same knowledge taught in any music classroom, expressed as rules the generator can follow.

Second, the conventions of game music itself. To make the output feel specifically like video game music, the rules lean into the patterns the genre is known for: melodies that mostly step to a nearby note and only occasionally leap, short repeating hooks, simple but strong chord progressions, and tight, punchy rhythms. Those conventions become weighted choices in the generator. For example, the melody steps to a nearby note most of the time and only occasionally jumps, because that is how most game melodies move.

The distinction that matters: because Bloop Maker is rule-based and deterministic rather than a model trained on existing songs, the music it produces is genuinely original. It is not remixing or recombining anyone's recordings, so there is no copyright-claim risk and nothing for a platform to flag as AI-generated content.

Why rules instead of AI?

Three reasons. First, ownership: because the music comes from code and not from a model trained on other people's songs, the output is unambiguously yours to use commercially. Second, control: rules are transparent and steerable, you can push a song bright, dark, cozy, punchy, cinematic, or lo-fi with a single button, and you always get the same result from the same seed. Third, independence: there's no server, no subscription, and no internet required. It all runs on your own computer, forever.

What kinds of songs can it make?

Bloop Maker covers a wide range of moods. Its modes go from calm and atmospheric ambient music, through balanced town and exploration themes, up to fast and intense battle music, plus jazzy, funky, silly, and downright weird flavors. On top of that you can pick from nine musical scales, each with its own feeling, and steer any song toward bright, dark, cozy, punchy, cinematic, or lo-fi with a single button. So while it is built for chiptune and video game music, the same engine can produce menu themes, boss fights, sad cutscene music, cozy shop loops, and more. And because everything exports as standard MIDI, you can take any idea into your own software and turn it into any style you like.

Bloop Maker vs AI music generators

AI music generators are trained on huge libraries of existing songs and try to predict the next note, which means three things you might not want: the output can echo the music it was trained on, raising copyright and "is this AI content?" questions; it usually needs an account, the cloud, or a subscription; and you cannot easily see or control why it made a choice. Bloop Maker is the opposite on every count. It is rule-based and deterministic, so the music is original and yours to keep. It runs entirely offline with no account and no subscription, as a one-time purchase. And because every choice follows a clear rule, you get the same song every time from the same seed, and you can steer and edit the result instead of rerolling and hoping. For game developers, streamers, and creators who need music that is safe to ship, that difference is the whole point.

What you do with the result

Once a song is generated you can edit it note by note in the built-in piano roll, reshape parts with one-click tools (simplify, swing, harmonize, reverse, and more), regenerate any single channel while keeping the rest, and then export the whole thing as a standard MIDI file for any DAW or render it straight to WAV audio using your own soundfonts.

Hearing your songs: using a SoundFont

Bloop Maker creates the music, and a SoundFont (.sf2) provides the actual instrument sounds. To hear audio, drag a .sf2 file into the Soundfont box in the app (or click the box to browse for one). Press play and your song comes to life through those instruments. You can swap soundfonts whenever you like to completely change the vibe, from retro 8-bit bleeps to a full orchestra, and in the full version you can give each channel its own soundfont.

Plenty of high-quality SoundFonts are free. Good ones to start with:

  • Arachno SoundFont (General MIDI, Roland GS)
  • GeneralUser GS (General MIDI, Roland GS)
  • Roland SC-55 (classic game-console sound)

You can also browse many more on Musical Artifacts and Soundfonts 4U.

One important note: the music you generate is yours, but each SoundFont is made by someone else and carries its own license. Exporting MIDI is always fine, but if you render audio (WAV) for a commercial project, check the license of the soundfont you used first. Full details on soundfont licensing here.
